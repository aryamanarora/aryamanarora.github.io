Aryaman Arora / Blog / Reorient interpretability towards improving the model training process

This blogpost is my attempt to understand the state of interpretability research and to figure out what is worth working on. This is a response to the widespread existential confusion in our field, as well as my own uncertainty about what to dedicate my efforts to. Neel Nanda thinks we should do pragmatic interpretability, Leo Gao thinks we should do ambitious interpretability, Jacob Steinhardt thinks we should do scalable end-to-end interpretability. Stephen Casper thinks interp research has been a waste, and frankly he has some good points that haven’t been satisfactorily addressed.

My basic argument is that, while interp has made some meaningful progress, the goal of comprehensively reverse-engineering behaviours has been misguided because reverse-engineering has not led to reliable interventions on model behaviour. Instead, non-interp interventions that have succeeded are done on decisions about training: data, architectures, and learning algorithms. Reorienting from the reverse-engineering agenda to a data/arch/learning-centric agenda is necessary to make progress on the thorny safety and controllability problems that we actually care about, and also to achieve meaningful scientific understanding that isn’t tricking ourselves.

I’ll argue using many pieces of evidence that existing justifications for the reverse-engineering agenda are not compelling, but I also claim that redirecting known techniques that have arisen from that agenda to a better agenda is possible. I hope this blogpost clears up some confusions and encourages more productive discussion in our field.

Judge interp by whether it lets us act on models

I subscribe to a interventionist notion of understanding: I think you should too! you can only claim to understand a phenomenon if you can apply an intervention which causes it to change. That is to say, an explanation of something is only valid if it makes correct predictions about what would happen in a counterfactual setting; if I had changed X, would Y have happened? Conversely, if I want Y to happen, what should I do to X?

In this view, understanding is about being able to do things which control the phenomenon of interest. The earliest hunter-gatherers understood fire in some sense, because they could make it and control it in order to do things like heat food. Current practitioners of language model training understand how to set up data and architecture to produce a model with certain capabilities, via scaling laws. The hunter-gatherers did not know what combustion is chemically, and language model trainers don’t know what mechanisms their model learns to achieve a particular test loss, but they both have an interventionist understanding of their target phenomenon.

Conversely, if someone claims to understand a phenomenon but fails to apply interventions which control it, we should be suspicious of their claim.

In the interp setting, this is something I’ll call the “causal turn”, which is exemplified by Atticus Geiger’s theory of causal abstraction. Geiger et al. (2025) is a great review, but there’s also earlier work like Geiger et al. (2021). Other people had similar ideas around the same time, if a bit less formalised; you might be interested in my 2023 blogpost on the causal turn in interp. This is also a mainstream position in philosophy of science. I’ve personally recently read Reutlinger et al. (2018), which has some compelling points about successful toy models providing interventional understanding to scientists. And Judea Pearl of course has several books on this topic. Claude tells me the key work on this topic is James Woodward’s Making Things Happen (2003), but I have not read it so I won’t make claims on that.

We should therefore ask of interpretability research: what can I now do to my models with interp that I couldn’t without it? I think the answer is: not that much, and I’ll tell you why.

Interp-based interventions for improving models have largely failed

In general, the strategy (stated or otherwise) of interp has been to reverse-engineer some undesirable phenomenon and come up with an inference-time patch which mitigates it. For example, enumerative safety is the idea that we can fully reverse-engineer a model, find the undesirable features/mechanisms, and disable them. For a more comprehensive treatment of and problems with this agenda, see this Charbel-Raphaël (2023) on the AI Alignment Forum and Lewis Smith (2024) on the “strong feature hypothesis”.

Even when they do not state so, inference-time interventions are usually what interp researchers have dedicated their efforts to, so I will take that as most of the field’s implicit theory of impact for interp.

Two techniques that are emblematic of interp-based solutions for undesirable behaviours are model editing and steering vectors, and they also serve as my evidence that these solutions have largely failed.

Model editing (or knowledge editing) proposes applying targeted weight-level modifications, exploiting the interp result that knowledge is localised to certain parts of the model. The fact that model editing is possible at all was surprising in the early days: early works like MEND and ROME, which target a subset of model parameters and are thus cheaper than standard finetuning, delivered impressive results. But model editing has fundamental issues: Nishi et al. (2025), for example, shows how representations can be destroyed by such edits in toy settings. And the original niche of model editing has been taken over by parameter-efficient finetuning methods, LoRA par excellence. On the data side, the limitations of model editing to account for multi-hop queries can be treated by augmenting data with LM rephrases. Clever insights about the weight geometry of models provided by interp cannot compete with gradient descent and good data. A related research problem, unlearning, has found similar conclusions.

Steering vectors are targeted representational interventions which exploit the interp result that computation is performed linearly in the token representation space of language models. Again, it’s surprising that many human-interpretable features are cleanly representated in models and can be intervened on to change behaviour. But more careful evaluation in work by Zhengxuan Wu and myself reveals that prompting (which takes advantage of LLM’s instruction-following capabilities) and finetuning (as above with model editing) offer more control over model behaviour than steering vectors in a comprehensive eval.

Relatedly, SAEs were proposed as a way to scalably learn the right decomposition of representation space into linear features in order to get a good dictionary of steering vectors, but SAE-based representation interventions have not been very successful either (again underperforming prompting and finetuning) despite an initial flashy demo by Anthropic which was pretty fun to try.

Finally, as far as outside researchers are aware, interp-based interventions have not been deployed in production AI systems. (I suspect some lab would have publicised it if they did.) Over the past half-decade, a great deal of interp research has touted its interventional applications, but we don’t have enough evidence for the success of such approaches.

However, interp-based monitoring has promise

Interp has, however, led to production deployment of probe monitors and classifiers which prevent undesirable outputs. Some really interesting publicised research on this has been put out by Anthropic recently, and is a concrete example of the practical application of interpretability for real-world problems. But probing as a technique predates the causal turn of interpretability research, and it (perhaps wisely?) does not aim to modify model behaviour. After many years of research, we would hope for more advances in monitoring, but our field has instead prioritised interventions with little success.

I think Transluce’s scalable end-to-end interpretability, which is accompanied by a broader push towards automated interpretability by various orgs, Transluce’s predictive concept decoders were concurrently released with Anthropic’s/MATs’s/some-ensemble-of-AI-safety-orgs’s activation oracles and essentially target the same task. EleutherAI, Goodfire, and multiple Anthropic teams are probably also working on similar things. is a good bet for scaling up interp-based monitoring beyond probes. There are also plausible applications of SAEs to this problem which I will discuss a bit later. However, these methods will be successful because they are not trying to patch the model after it’s been trained; they are playing to the strengths of interpretability techniques which we have practically observed.

Still, even if interp-based interventions have not been successful, we have broadly improved our ability to control model behaviour even as capabilities advance. If not through interp, what research has led to this progress? I argue that all the research that has worked out has focused on improving the next iteration of models, and not inference-time patching, via: data, architecture, and learning algorithms.

Techniques which improve data, architecture, and learning algorithms beat post-hoc interventions

Despite interp having little contribution to directly improving models, models have nonetheless improved in both capabilities and safety. Why? It’s pretty clear that this is because of improvements in data, architecture, and learning algorithms.

I won’t go into capabilities improvements in depth because there are too many to list: LM-aided data filtering and augmentation, midtraining on high-quality data, mixture-of-experts architectures, gated MLPs, figuring out where to put norms, the Muon optimiser, the rise of reinforcement learning for verifiable tasks and the curation of environments for that purpose, etc.

On the safety side, I think the exciting progress has come from similar approaches. First, Anthropic’s constitutional AI, where a constitution of desired values is used to iteratively self improve a system’s alignment, has been unexpectedly successful. Somewhat relatedly, OpenAI’s deliberative alignment used RL to make models reason about safety specifications to better control their behaviour.

There’s been conceptual progress in understanding the personas that assistant-trained AI systems adopt: Janus’s simulators and nostalgebraist’s void, for example, and it leads us to believe that models latch on to personas from their (underspecified) training data, explaining weird phenomena like emergent misalignment, broadly quirky generalisation in finetuning, and that pretraining data containing instances of harmful AI personas is part of what makes models misaligned. And it even seems that character training via constitutional AI is far more robust than steering vectors.

Overall, it seems like the shape of things which are helping our ability to control model behaviour is very similar to that of any other capability: we must shape the data, the architecture, and/or the learning algorithm. Nothing like “intervene on the model after it’s been trained” succeeded.

Why hasn’t reverse-engineering helped us make better training decisions so far?

I think it’s worth discussing why reverse-engineering research has not led to contributions towards data, architecture, and learning algorithm improvements. I do think a large cause of this is simply research culture: not enough researcher time and thought has been dedicated towards this application. But there may also be some fundamental problems in the reverse-engineering agenda itself.

Reverse engineering finds artifacts but not actionable causes. When we interp researchers find the circuit underlying some behaviour, we are making a claim about inference-time interventions on the model; we evaluate circuits by ablating particular representations. For example, Marks et al. (2025) propose SHIFT as a technique for debiasing models based on the results of circuit tracing, and the Mechnistic Interpretability Benchmark is interested in the inference-time intervention capabilities of circuit-tracing methods. We know that this approach is brittle, as I argued above. But even if we are confident in the circuit that we have found, we still don’t have a lever on training data, architecture, or learning algorithm to modify the behaviour described by the circuit. It might be possible to do this, but perhaps due to a lack of imagination this has not been really tried.

Reverse engineering is poorly predictive, either due to limitations in our methods or the fundamental messiness of model representations. We often study simple data distributions when reverse engineering the behaviour of an AI system, and find that our results don’t generalise enough for interventional robustness (even compared to e.g. finetuning on the same data). For example, much work in causal abstraction examines token-aligned counterfactual pairs, and it has been difficult to extend this to complex behaviours where such counterfactuals do not exist. But consider work by my friend Jiuding Sun on HyperSteer, which works towards non-token-aligned counterfactual interventions. Another potential issue in our techniques is that we are simply making the wrong assumptions about feature geometry; maybe the linear representation hypothesis is insufficient, and SAEs with a different geometric inductive bias would have succeeded.

Despite all this, the pessimistic (yes!) alternative is that models are fundamentally a bag of heuristics which a human being would never hypothesise looking for. For example, the addition circuit found in LMs by Nikankin et al. (2024) is a messy digit-wise set of heuristics and not a clean algorithm like the ones we would think to look for. And in this case, failures will be unsystematic and there will not be a compressed description of the model’s internal algorithm for us to extrapolate from. A bag of heuristics is something gradient descent can operate on much better than a human being!

The beautiful results we find may be about the task, not the model. When you get two Claudes to talk to each other, they often fall into an attractor state where they gush about blissfulness and enlightenment. Similarly, when you get two interp researchers to talk to each other, they’ll probably gush about all the beautiful feature geometry we’ve discovered: circular arrangements of days of the week, helices of numbers, manifolds for character counting, etc. While it’s undeniable (to me, at least) that these and other scientifically-minded work in mech interp has resulted in beautiful findings, I fear these are not telling us anything useful about the models but are rather simply describing the appropriate solution to the language modelling subtask that any model would have learned. That is to say, I think much of what is surprising and interesting about the internal computations performed by models is an artifact of the structure of the data, and doesn’t help us make progress on improving models. Evidence for this: different architectures seek to learn similar algorithms, e.g. induction heads show up in SSM variants just as they do in Transformers. This is part of what we found in Arora et al. (2025): different architectures (Transformers, Mamba, etc.) converge on similar solutions to associative recall despite calling on different components to perform the steps of the algorithm. We also historically just have less understanding about what kinds of algorithms autogressive language modelling would require, compared to chunk-by-chunk processing (consider linguistic theory and its role in NLP in the pre-scaling era), so we should expect to be surprised due to our lack of experience with this task.

Overall, we end up with techniques that propose cute post-hoc descriptions of internal mechanisms but which fail to provide guidance on how to change the model’s behaviour in a robust way.

Conclusion: What does useful interp look like?

The lesson is clear: we must conduct interp research towards the end of changing the data models are trained on, what their architecture is, and what learning algorithms we use. I’ve ordered these based on what I think interp has the most to contribute to.

First, there is overwhelming evidence that data is an underexploited lever for changing model behaviour. Besides the examples I gave above, I’ll give you two specific examples of interp intersecting with data:

  • Neil Rathi just put out a paper on shaping capabilities with data filtering, which uses SAE features to train a probe which is used to filter out undesirable data from pretraining, with excellent results on downstream tasks compared to post-hoc mitigations (even with finetuning).
  • Nick Jiang has nice work on data-centric interp, which uses SAEs as essentially interpretable embeddings for analysing differences in datasets (e.g. the traits of two LMs). It’s not difficult to imagine applications of this approach to data filtering.

This data-focused interp idea has been in the water for a while, at least since influence functions were proposed for neural networks. It’s therefore surprising that the conceptual leap from finding causes of behaviours in the data to interventions on that data has been lacking in interp research.

On architectures, I’ll give a clear example of interp contributing to architectural improvements, but with the twist that no interp researchers actually did the application — they only provided the findings! When Anthropic discovered induction heads and their relation to in-context learning in Transformers, researchers working on state-space models found out that their existing architectures failed on simple induction tasks: Fu et al. (2022) introduced H3, an architecture which incorporates a shift-SSM component in order to induce a an equivalent of the previous-token heads in Transformers. Later SSM research continued benchmarking on this task and started using short convolutions for this instead. Surprisingly, there hasn’t been interp work investigating this connection or looking for ways to improve efficient architectures via similar insights — I believe that is a huge missed opportunity.

On learning algorithms, I don’t have a great example to offer so far, but the kinds of innovation I find interesting are about the optimisation process and reward functions. I hope more interp work looks at those instead of just repeating work on “chain-of-thought has a lot of tokens so it’s hard to find circuits on it”, which has dubious future applications based on my arguments above. One interesting direction is metagradients (i.e. backprop through training), which has been applied to influence functions; in general, interp on gradients feels like an underexplored direction which may help us with monitoring and prevented unexpected behaviour during training, where behavioural auditing may be too slow or noisy.

Conclusion

If you are an interp researcher, I think you should reorient your work towards figuring out how to shape data, architecture, and learning algorithms via interp, and not just work on reverse-engineering a random behaviour. Consider techniques dealing with (1) data attribution, (2) architecture design and analysis, and (3) training-time monitoring.

A (justified) response I expect to this post is that mech interp is pre-paradigmatic — we’re waiting for a conceptual breakthrough, e.g. finding the right primitives for features, which will make the unsuccessful interventions work. While I am sympathetic to this claim (I think reverse-engineering is tractable in theory), the track record of the last 5 years has made me skeptical that this is going to happen anytime soon. I instead argue that there are useful things we can do with reverse-engineering-based techniques right now, which do not involve a wholesale rejection of mechanistic interpretability and ca give us a better perspective on our research programme.
